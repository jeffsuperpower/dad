# Research Caption Writing Guide

## Hook

- Lead with the finding, never the study design. "Muscle power is a far stronger predictor of death than muscle strength" not "A study of 3,889 adults found that..."
- The hook should be one sentence, ideally under 20 words
- It should make someone stop scrolling because it's surprising, counterintuitive, or raises immediate stakes
- If a trial failed, that can be the hook. "A fiber trial with 802 prediabetic adults failed" is more interesting than "fiber works differently for everyone"
- Don't use triplet/staccato openers (e.g. "45 million. 196 countries. 3,236 data points.") as these feel formulaic and are an AI giveaway
- The hook earns the right to the detail that follows. If the hook doesn't land, nothing else matters

## Structure & Flow

- Build an arc: finding > scale/evidence > specific data > stakes/closer
- Each paragraph should be 1-2 sentences. Never more than 3
- Don't be too stop-starty or choppy. Combine sentences where the flow is natural. "Researchers analyzed 11,115 gut samples from 39 countries" reads better as one sentence than two
- Break bulky paragraphs apart. If a paragraph has more than two distinct ideas, split it or use a dot point list
- Use dot point lists when you have 3+ parallel items (diseases, biomarkers, outcomes, mechanisms). Lists are easier to scan and hit harder than cramming items into a comma-separated sentence
- Don't over-nest information. If a sentence requires a parenthetical inside a clause inside another clause, break it out
- End with a punchy closer, ideally one line that reframes or raises stakes without editorializing. The closer should feel like a full stop, not a sermon

## Tone

- Factual, clean, no commentary or editorializing. Let the data do the talking
- Don't tell people how to feel. Present the finding, let them react
- No poetic or AI-sounding lines. "Our biology hasn't caught up" and "We engineered the food supply for shelf life, not human health" both sound like they were written by a machine trying to be profound. If you wouldn't say it in conversation, don't write it
- No motivational or preachy closers. "Your health is in your hands" is a bumper sticker, not a caption
- Conversational but not casual. You can say "your gut bacteria" but not "this is wild"
- When hedging, use small precise words: "roughly", "may", "approximately", "~". Don't over-qualify. One hedge per claim is enough
- Match the tone throughout. If the first four paragraphs are clean and factual, the closer can't suddenly become philosophical

## Formatting

- No dashes (em dashes or en dashes). Use commas or periods instead
- No emojis
- No hashtags
- No triplet openers
- Dot point lists: use a simple dash or bullet, no bold, no sub-bullets
- Keep URLs at the very end, on their own line
- No title/header above the caption

## Accuracy & Framing

- Every caption gets checked against the actual paper before posting. Invoke `marcus-medical-reviewer` for verification. No exceptions
- Never present a post-hoc finding as if it were the main result. If the trial's primary endpoint failed and the interesting finding came from subgroup analysis, say so. The failure can be the hook
- Don't merge findings from two different studies into one claim. If the 50% figure came from a 3-day study and the 7-day persistence came from a single-day study, keep them separate
- Use precise medical terminology: "chronic fatigue syndrome" not "chronic fatigue" (one is a diagnosis, the other is a symptom). "Genus" not "bacterium" when referring to a group of 300+ strains
- When a percentage is a relative risk reduction, be aware that it is, even if you don't spell that out in the caption. Never present relative risk reduction in a way that could be mistaken for absolute reduction
- Don't overstate causal claims. Observational studies show associations, not causation. "Linked to" and "associated with" are different from "causes" and "prevents"
- "Your microbiome decides" is too deterministic. "Your microbiome may influence" is accurate. One word can be the difference between credible and clickbait
- Watch for confidence intervals. If a study reports HR 6.9 with a 95% CI of 1.61 to 29.58, the point estimate is dramatic but the uncertainty is enormous. Don't present it as a precise number without knowing the CI is tight enough to support it
- If the study had no control group, say so or at minimum don't present it as a definitive finding. n=14 with no control is a pilot study, not proof
- If the paper is a review (not original research), the claims trace back to underlying studies with their own limitations. Don't present review conclusions with the confidence of a primary RCT
- Don't conflate methodologies you're not sure about. "Epigenetic clock" and "blood biomarker composite" are different things. Check the paper
- If something was inferred from genomic analysis rather than observed in a lab, note that distinction. "Genetic analysis suggests it produces vitamin B12" is different from "it produces vitamin B12"
- If an author uses the word "candidate" or "may" or "appears to", those hedges are there for a reason. Don't drop them

## What Makes a Strong Post

- A clear, striking finding that stands on its own without needing a paragraph of context
- Real numbers that hit hard: 1 in 3, 5.9x, 1.54 billion, 40% lower risk
- A "why should I care" thread running through it. The reader should feel the stakes without being told them
- Specificity over abstraction. "Colorectal cancer, Parkinson's, MS" hits harder than "13 diseases." Names are visceral, numbers are abstract
- When you can have both the number and the names, do both: "depleted in people across 13 different diseases, including colorectal cancer, Parkinson's, MS, and IBD"
- The closer should add something: either reframe the finding, raise the stakes, or point to what's still unknown. Don't just restate what was already said

## What Makes a Weak Post

- A mid-tier finding forced into dramatic framing. If the study is underwhelming, either find a more interesting angle or skip it
- A caption that's technically accurate but buries the lead three paragraphs deep
- AI-sounding philosophical closers that break the factual tone of everything above them
- Overly long captions that could lose a third of their words without losing any information
- Claims that sound revolutionary but collapse under scrutiny (trial failed its primary endpoint, n=14, no control group, key biomarker didn't actually change)

## Length

- Aim for 4-6 short paragraphs or paragraph-equivalent blocks (including any dot point lists)
- If a caption feels long, it probably is. Cut the weakest paragraph first and see if anything is lost
- The test: if you removed a paragraph and the caption still makes sense and still lands, that paragraph wasn't needed
- Don't pad with context that doesn't serve the reader. Study design details (randomized, open-label, UK Biobank) earn their place only if they add credibility or are necessary to understand the finding

## Before Publishing Checklist

1. Does the hook make someone stop scrolling?
2. Is every claim verified against the actual paper (via Marcus)?
3. Are there any dashes, emojis, or triplet openers?
4. Does the tone stay consistent from hook to closer?
5. Is the closer earned, or does it sound like an AI wrote a motivational quote?
6. Could any paragraph be cut without losing the thread?
7. Would a bulky section read better as a dot point list?
8. Are all medical terms precise (syndrome vs symptom, genus vs bacterium, association vs causation)?
9. Is any finding presented more definitively than the paper actually supports it?
10. Is the URL at the end on its own line?
