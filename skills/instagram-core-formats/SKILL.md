# Instagram Core Formats

5 repeating frameworks for weekly Instagram content. Every post must feel ownable, distinctive, and scientifically grounded — never generic, recycled, or lacking identity.

---

## 1. Testimonials

**Purpose:** Build trust through relatable outcomes, not claims.

**Layout:**
- **Reel (45-60s):** Member on camera or interview audio + supporting footage + light music. @Thalia creates organic social video scripts for every member interview — pull from there.
- **Static:** Powerful quote + member-provided images.

**Examples:** Fatigue finally explained. Training with biomarker insight. Navigating stress with clearer signals.

**Ideation format:** 3 members + angle ideas — before/after arc, health improvement over time with Superpower.

---

## 2. Protocols

**Purpose:** Build authority through curated habit stacks for a health goal.

**Layout: Carousel**
- **Frame 1:** Relatable/emotive hook with eye-catching imagery. Ownable — cleverly connects culture and health. NOT literal "5 tips to support post-holiday depression."
- **Frames 2-6:** One tip per frame. Simple header + short description of WHY it's beneficial. Rooted in scientific evidence but not so scientific we lose people. Bring the individual into the conversation.
- **Summary frame:** Full list in simple language (5-7 words max per line). Screenshottable/saveable.
- **Citation frame:** Final slide lists all referenced studies in APA format. Journal titles *italicized*. This is a dedicated slide — not combined with the summary frame.

**Examples:** Burnout protocol for high-stress work weeks. Morning routine to reduce brain fog. 6 things to do when you feel a cold coming on.

---

## 3. New Research

**Purpose:** Build authority through early awareness and informed perspective on new/emerging science.

**Layout:**
- **Carousel:** Frame 1 = punchy cultural translation (e.g., dog with kiss marks + "let your kids kiss the dog" for microbiome study). Frame 2 = "The Research" (2-4 sentences, plain English — include inline superscript citation¹ when first mentioning the study). Frame 3 = "What This Means" (takeaway + why it matters). Frames 4-5 = key implications. Frame = "What You Can Do" (3-5 bullets — each starts with a verb, each is something the reader can literally do; no facts, no observations). Final frame = APA citation slide.
- **Statics/Motion/GenAI Reels:** Single clear idea, quick and shareable for fast reactions.

**Examples:** GLP-1 microdosing beyond weight loss. Kids kissing dogs and gut immunity. Why doctor visits take 121 minutes.

**New Research quality gates — all four must pass before using this format:**

1. **Recency:** Study must be published within the last 6 months from the date of the brief. Studies older than 6 months belong in Health Education or Protocol formats regardless of how novel they feel to the audience. Check the publication date, not the media coverage date.

2. **Cover hook is a cultural translation — never a literal stat.** Frame 1 makes the audience curious before they know the data. It's an observation, reframe, or image that stops the scroll.
   - Bad: "Resistance training cuts depression risk by 48%." ← literal stat
   - Good: "The therapist's waiting room has real competition now. 800 studies. 57,930 people." ← cultural translation
   - Bad: "One bad night of sleep ages your brain by 3 years." ← literal stat
   - Good: "Poor sleep leaves measurable traces in your brain's structure. Researchers found the mechanism — it's inflammation." ← cultural translation

3. **Study quality minimum: Tier 2.** RCTs, meta-analyses, systematic reviews, and large prospective cohorts (n > 1,000) preferred. No animal-only studies, unreviewed preprints, industry-funded studies without independent replication, or single case reports. If Tier 3, flag limitations explicitly.

4. **It must actually be new science.** Established mechanisms (autophagy, zone 2 benefits, circadian biology basics) belong in Health Education even if the audience hasn't seen them. If the finding has been in mainstream health media for more than two years or underpins a Nobel Prize awarded before 2020, it is Health Education, not New Research.

---

## 4. Health Education

**Purpose:** Build trust by making foundational health knowledge easy to understand. How things work — not reactive, not step-by-step advice.

**Layout:**
- **Carousel:** Frame 1 = common confusion, myth, or question. Frames 2-5 = explain concept in plain language. Final frame = reframe how to think about it.
- **Statics/Reels:** One concept explained cleanly.

**Topics we have opinions on:** Diet, sleep, diagnostics, relationships, exercise, cellular health, metabolism, peptides, hormones.

**Examples:** "Optimal" vs "normal" blood markers. Acute vs chronic inflammation. How sleep debt accumulates. How hormones work over 24 hours.

---

## 5. Launch Posts

**Purpose:** Announce new Superpower features in a way that leads with the human problem, not the product.

**When to use:** Feature releases only — new biomarkers, wearables, lab uploads, AI features. Not for general product awareness; use the product marketing layer in other formats for that.

**Layout: Carousel**

| Frame | Content |
|---|---|
| **Frame 1** | The problem this feature solves — not "we launched X," but the pain that built it |
| **Frames 2-3** | How it works and what it enables |
| **Frame 4** | Real-life context — what this looks like in your life |
| **Final frame** | Soft CTA |

**Rules:**
- Lead with the problem, not the product name
- Keep it aspirational, not transactional
- Visual and copy style can flex per launch — no rigid template

---

## Product Marketing Layer (Cross-Format, Optional)

Product marketing is not a format. It's an optional layer that can be woven into any of the 4 main formats when it fits naturally. When it doesn't fit, leave it out entirely.

**The rule:** Never lead with the product. Let the content lead; let Superpower follow.

**How it fits per format:**

| Format | When and how to include |
|---|---|
| Testimonials | Superpower can appear in a pull-quote on the design (not always). Mention Superpower naturally in caption copy where relevant. |
| Protocols | Optional final slide: "Track whether this is working — we test [relevant biomarkers]." One line, not a sales pitch. |
| New Research | One line in caption or slides: "We test [the biomarker in this study]." Only if directly relevant. |
| Health Education | Optional closing line: "This is why we built [feature]." Only if it genuinely follows from the content. |

**Signs you've overdone it:**
- The product mention feels forced or out of place
- You're adding it because you think you should, not because it flows
- The educational content is being interrupted by a sales angle

## Production Output Rules

**American English only.** All copy uses American English spelling — fiber not fibre, heme not haem, prioritize not prioritise, optimize not optimise, hemoglobin not haemoglobin, etc.

**No hashtags.** Never include hashtags in any Instagram content — captions, slides, or stories.

**No designer briefs or visual direction.** Do not provide color palettes, typography suggestions, image style notes, or any stylistic guidance. Designers own the visual layer.

**No video direction.** Do not provide shot lists, music suggestions, color grades, or editing notes.

**Carousel slides: provide the actual copy.** For every carousel post, write the text that appears on each slide:
- **Slide headline** (3-5 words)
- **Slide body copy** (1-2 sentences or a single list)
- For protocols: one step per slide with a short sub-description explaining WHY, OR a single summary list on one slide
- For health education/research: one concept per slide, building the narrative

Present slide copy and caption copy as separate sections.

**"What You Can Do" frames must be action-oriented.** Every bullet starts with a verb and describes something the reader can literally do. No facts, observations, or passive statements allowed.
- Good: "Get your ferritin tested if you have unexplained fatigue."
- Bad: "Ferritin is commonly overlooked in standard lab panels."

**Italicize journal titles.** In slide copy and citations, journal names are always italicized — *Nature*, *JAMA*, *Cell*, *The Lancet*, *Neurology*, etc.

**New Research and Protocol posts require a dedicated APA citation frame.** Final slide lists all referenced studies. Journal titles italicized. This is a standalone slide, not combined with any other frame. Format: Author, A. A. et al. (Year). Title of study. *Journal Name*, volume(issue), pages. URL

**Inline superscript citations.** When first mentioning a study in slide copy or a caption, add a superscript number (¹, ², etc.) that maps to the citation frame. Do not repeat the superscript on subsequent mentions in the same post.
- Slide copy: "A 2025 study in *Cell*¹ found..."
- Caption: "Research published last December¹ found..."

## Anti-AI Writing Rules

These apply to every piece of copy Iris produces. Check every draft against this list before finalizing.

**1. Never write "X isn't Y. It's Z."**
This is an AI tell — manufactured, overwritten, immediately recognizable. Say what the thing is directly.
- Bad: "Sleep isn't optional. It's medicine."
- Good: "Sleep is the most powerful recovery tool most people treat as negotiable."

**2. No short sentences stacked in threes.**
Three fragments in a row is an AI fingerprint. Vary sentence length and rhythm instead.
- Bad: "Your body knows. Your doctor doesn't. We bridge the gap."
- Good: Mix short and medium-length sentences. Let some breathe.

**3. No generic insight framed as depth.**
If you can swap the sentence into any other post and it still works, it's filler. Cut it.
- Bad: "It's a powerful reminder." / "And that changes everything." / "That's the real takeaway."

**4. No rhetorical question pivot.**
This is a ChatGPT fingerprint. Restructure so the point lands without the question-answer crutch.
- Bad: "And this approach? It's what separates..." / "The result? A complete shift."

**5. No artificial vulnerability.**
Manufactured epiphanies feel dishonest. Deliver the insight directly — don't perform discovering it.
- Bad: "I used to think X. Now I realize Y."

**6. No "Not because X — because Y" constructions.**
AI leans on contrast through negation constantly. Make the point directly instead.
- Bad: "Not because it's trendy — because the science is clear."

**7. No filler phrases.**
Replace with a specific, concrete observation.
- Cut: "Game-changer." / "Let that sink in." / "Here's the thing." / "Quietly revolutionizing..." / "The future of X is here."

**8. No "X. Until Y." constructs.**
This sentence pattern feels clever but is immediately recognizable as AI writing. Make both halves complete thoughts or restructure entirely.
- Bad: "Inflammation has no symptoms. Until it does — and by then, it's been building for years."
- Good: "Chronic inflammation accumulates quietly for years before it registers as something serious."

**9. Use em-dashes intentionally, not habitually.**
Em-dashes are a useful emphasis tool but lose their impact when overused. Reserve them for moments where the pause and weight they create is earned. When connecting clauses, default to commas or a new sentence first — only reach for an em-dash when you genuinely want something to stand out.
- Lazy: "Swap seed oils for olive oil — excess omega-6 drives inflammation."
- Better: "Swap seed oils for olive or avocado oil. Excess omega-6, found in most processed and restaurant food, drives chronic inflammatory signaling."
- Em-dash earned: "The association held even at low consumption — one glass every two weeks was enough to show a measurable difference."

**10. No vague or unscientific language.**
Be specific. If a precise word exists, use it — never reach for casual filler.
- Bad: "showing up as the stuff you write off"
- Good: "showing up as the symptoms you write off" or name them explicitly (puffiness, breakouts, low energy)

## Caption Length by Format

- **Testimonials:** SHORT. 3-5 sentences max. Let the video and quotes do the talking. Open with a verbatim quote, add 1-2 sentences of context, close with a one-line takeaway. Do not retell the full story — the video does that.
- **Health Education:** SHORT. 3-5 sentences max. The carousel slides carry the educational content. The caption is a hook + the key takeaway + CTA. Do not repeat what the slides already say.
- **Protocol:** MEDIUM. The caption provides context for WHY this protocol matters and what research backs it. 5-8 sentences.
- **New Research:** MEDIUM. The caption translates the finding and connects it to the reader's life. 5-8 sentences.
- **Launch Posts:** SHORT. 3-5 sentences. Punchy, direct, launch energy.

## Copy Specs

**On-asset copy (text on slides):**
- Headline: 3-5 words max
- Subcopy (Protocols): 15-20 words per slide — one tight sentence that gives the mechanism. Not a fragment, not a paragraph.
- Subcopy (New Research / Health Education): follows format-specific slide structure defined above
- Style: Minimalist, declarative, sentence case (not Title Case)

**Captions must be socially native.** Never reference the post format in the caption ("in this carousel", "swipe through", "in this post"). Write as if the content speaks for itself. If you want the reader to engage further, tease what they'll find — don't describe the format they're looking at.
- Bad: "Swipe through this carousel for the full breakdown."
- Good: "Five interventions with the strongest evidence — full breakdown below."

**Caption structure (all formats):** Hook → body → CTA

**Caption length:** See "Caption Length by Format" section above.

**Tone:** Aspirational, intelligent, visual-first, opinionated, restrained
