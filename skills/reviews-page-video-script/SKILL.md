# Reviews Page Video Script

## What This Is

A skill for writing 90-120 second testimonial video scripts for superpower.com/reviews. These are verbatim-only scripts cut from recorded interviews — the editor will assemble real audio, so every word must exist in the transcript.

**This is NOT:**
- A paid ad script (those are 30-45 seconds, hook-driven — use `testimonial-extraction-paid-video`)
- A UGC script with talent reading lines (use `video-script-writing`)
- A blog or email extraction (use `testimonial-extraction-organic`)

**This IS:**
- One person's complete health journey, told in their own words
- 90-120 seconds of continuous, flowing narrative
- Something you'd watch on a reviews page and think: "that sounds like me"

---

## The Problem This Skill Solves

Without this skill, scripts come out as:
- **Choppy quote blocks** under [OPEN] / [TURN] / [CLOSE] headers that read like outlines
- **Too many threads** — trying to cover cardiovascular + fertility + inflammation + bio age in 90 seconds
- **Too little material** — 4-5 quotes with nothing to cut from or rearrange
- **Degrading on iteration** — each round of feedback makes it worse because quotes get reshuffled instead of the script being rewritten

This skill fixes all of that.

## Process

### Step 1: Read the Full Transcript and Find the ONE Story

Before touching a single quote, read the entire transcript and answer:

**What is the single strongest narrative thread?**

A narrative thread is ONE clear through-line with a beginning, middle, and end. Not two threads. Not three. One.

Ask yourself:
- What is the most emotionally compelling part of this person's story?
- What would make a stranger stop and watch?
- What's the ONE thing they discovered that changed how they think about their health?

**Good narrative threads:**
- "I have a family history of heart disease but never thought it applied to me. Then a calcium score at 39 proved it did." (Alexandra)
- "I work in fertility but my own doctor wouldn't give me fertility testing. Superpower removed that barrier — and now I'm planning my family." (Camelia)
- "My doctor said I was fine for years. Superpower found I was pre-diabetic." (hypothetical)

**Bad narrative threads (too many things):**
- "She had perimenopause AND cardiovascular risk AND inflammation AND brain fog" — pick ONE
- "He found low vitamin D AND high cholesterol AND was motivated to retest" — pick the one with the most emotional weight

**How to choose when there are multiple strong threads:**
1. Which one has the clearest emotional arc (before → surprise → after)?
2. Which one has the best quotes — the most specific, vivid, quotable language?
3. Which one would resonate with the broadest audience?

Write one sentence describing the narrative thread before proceeding:
> "This is the story of [name] who [situation] and discovered [revelation] which meant [stakes]."

---

### Step 2: Pull 10-15 Candidate Quotes

Now scan the transcript for every quote that serves your chosen narrative thread. Pull generously — you want 10-15 quotes, which is MORE than you'll use. This gives the user material to cut from and rearrange.

For each quote, note:
- The verbatim text (exact words, including filler)
- The line number(s)
- Where it sits in the story arc (setup, escalation, climax, resolution)

**What makes a good quote for this format:**
- It moves the story forward (not a tangent)
- It could be understood by someone with no context about Superpower
- It carries emotional weight — you can hear the person's voice
- It's specific, not generic ("my calcium score was 15 out of 400" beats "the results were concerning")

**Don't pull:**
- Interviewer questions (obviously)
- Quotes that require explanation to understand
- Generic praise for Superpower that doesn't advance the story
- Quotes from tangential topics outside your chosen thread

---

### Step 3: Write the Long First Draft (~150-180 Seconds)

Now write the script. This is the most important step and where previous attempts failed.

#### Rules for the Draft:

**1. Write it as continuous flowing speech.**
The output should read like one person sitting across from you telling their story. Not a slide deck. Not an outline. Not quote blocks under headers.

**2. Write LONG — 150-180 seconds worth.**
This is deliberately longer than the 90-120 second target. The user needs material to cut from. It's much easier to trim a good long draft than to pad a thin short one.

At ~150 words per minute of natural speech:
- 90 seconds ≈ 225 words
- 120 seconds ≈ 300 words
- 150 seconds ≈ 375 words
- 180 seconds ≈ 450 words

Aim for 375-450 words in the first draft.

**3. Every word must be verbatim from the transcript.**
This is an edit guide for cutting together recorded audio. You cannot write transitions, bridges, or connecting sentences that the person didn't actually say. If there's a jump between quotes, note it for the editor — but the spoken words must be real.

**4. Arrange quotes for narrative flow, not chronological order.**
You can pull from anywhere in the transcript. A quote from line 302 can come before a quote from line 52 if it tells a better story. The editor will handle the cuts.

**5. Build an emotional arc.**
Every good 90-second story has:
- **Grounding** — Who is this person? Why should I care? What's the context?
- **Escalation** — Something builds. Tension, curiosity, concern.
- **Climax** — The moment of revelation, surprise, or emotional peak.
- **Resolution** — How they feel now. What it means. Why it matters.

The climax should land roughly 2/3 to 3/4 through the script. Don't put the best moment at the end where it feels rushed — and don't put it at the beginning where there's nothing to build toward.

**6. Use line numbers inline, not as section breaks.**
Format: Put the line reference at the end of each quote block, in parentheses. Like this:

```
"I have heart issues on both sides of my family. My grandfather had like eight heart attacks. My father has stints in his heart."
(lines 301-302)

"But I never really took it super seriously. You're just like — oh, my grandfather's old, that happens to people."
(line 302)
```

Not like this:
```
**[OPEN — Family History]**
> "I have heart issues..."
> *(Line 301)*
```

**7. Light editorial notes only where needed.**
If there's a jump the editor needs to handle, or footage from different interviews that shouldn't be mixed, note it briefly in brackets. Don't over-annotate.

---

### Step 4: Add Editor Notes at the Bottom

After the script, include a short section with:
- Which interview(s) the footage comes from (if multiple exist)
- Where the emotional peak is (so the editor knows to let it breathe)
- Any footage constraints (e.g., "don't mix Interview 1 and Interview 2 outfits")
- B-roll opportunities if obvious from the story

Keep this to 4-6 bullet points max.

---

### Step 5: Revision Rules

When the user gives feedback:

**REWRITE THE FULL SCRIPT FROM SCRATCH.**

Do not:
- Swap quote blocks around
- Patch in new quotes to the existing structure
- Try to preserve parts of the old draft

Do:
- Re-read the feedback carefully
- Understand what narrative direction they want
- Identify which quotes serve that direction
- Write a fresh, flowing draft that incorporates ALL their notes
- If they shared specific quotes they want included, weave those in naturally

The reason scripts degrade on iteration is that patching creates Frankenstein drafts. A clean rewrite from the feedback is always better.

```markdown
## [Member Name] — Reviews Page Video Script

**Duration:** ~[X] seconds (long draft for trimming)
**Source:** [Which interview(s), any footage constraints]

---

[The script as continuous flowing speech, with line numbers in parentheses after each quote block. No section headers within the script unless absolutely necessary for the editor to understand a major shift.]

---

**Editor notes:**
- [4-6 bullets with footage guidance, emotional peaks, constraints]
```

## Gold Standard Example

This is the approved script for Alexandra Nolan. All future scripts should match this quality and flow.

```markdown
## Alexandra Nolan — Reviews Page Video Script

**Narrative thread:** Family history of heart disease she dismissed her whole life — until a calcium score at 39 revealed it was already starting.
**Duration:** ~100 seconds
**Source:** Interview 2 only (different outfit from Interview 1 — do not mix footage)

---

"There are heart issues on both sides of my family. My grandfather on my mom's side had like eight heart attacks in his life. My father has stints in his heart."
(lines 301-302)

"But I never really took it super seriously. You're just like — oh, my grandfather's old, that happens to people. Oh, my dad's old. You just don't process the fact that it can be hereditary."
(line 302)

"I found out I was in perimenopause at 39 and that's what really made me stop and think — if that's happening outward, how long has it been going on? And what else is happening inside that I can't really see?"
(lines 52-54)

"I did the Superpower test and saw that my cholesterol was at the high-risk level. My doctor said, you're kind of young for these numbers. She suggested I go to a cardiologist and get a calcium score."
(lines 305-307)

"Even the cardiologist said — I'm sure your calcium score is going to be zero. You're only 39. And when it wasn't..."
(line 307)

"It was 15 out of 400. I get it, that seems low. But that means there is hard calcium buildup in my arteries right now. And what's more concerning — the hard plaque comes from soft plaque. That's the silent killer. That's the thing that can float around and clog an artery and can kill you."
(lines 307-309)

"If I didn't do this, I could be 49, 50 and have all of these issues — when all I had to do was be proactive about my health, test those biomarkers, and get a plan in place a decade before."
(lines 313-314)

---

**Editor notes:**
- Use Interview 2 footage ONLY (different outfit from Interview 1 — do not mix)
- The calcium score reveal (line 307) is the emotional peak — let that moment breathe
- The "silent killer" section (line 309) should feel sobering, not sensational
- Close should feel empowered, not scared
```

### What Makes This Work:

1. **One thread:** Family history → dismissed it → got tested → calcium score surprise → soft plaque → now she knows. Nothing about inflammation, brain fog, bio age, or supplements.
2. **Flows like speech:** You can read it straight through and it sounds like one person talking. No jarring jumps.
3. **Emotional build:** Starts casual (dismissing family history), builds concern (perimenopause, cholesterol), peaks at the calcium score reveal, resolves with empowerment.
4. **More than minimum:** 7 quote blocks gives the user and editor material to work with. Easy to trim to 90 seconds by cutting one or two blocks.
5. **Line numbers are helpful, not intrusive:** They're there for the editor but don't break up the reading flow.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Too many threads | Trying to be comprehensive | Commit to ONE thread before pulling any quotes |
| Too short | Being concise feels "good" | Write long first (150-180 sec), let user trim |
| Choppy blocks | Using structural headers as a crutch | Write flowing speech, use line numbers inline |
| Gets worse on revision | Patching instead of rewriting | Always rewrite from scratch on feedback |
| Generic close | Running out of steam at the end | The close should be one of the strongest quotes — pick it early |
| Over-annotating | Trying to be helpful | Editor notes at the bottom only, 4-6 bullets max |
